Supply & Demand

The invisible hand: how markets find prices without anyone deciding.

The Market as a Computer

How does a city with millions of people get fed? No one plans it. No central authority decides how many apples or how much bread to produce. Yet somehow, stores have roughly what people want to buy. This coordination emerges from supply and demand—the most powerful idea in economics.

Price as Information

Prices are signals. When something is scarce, its price rises, telling producers "make more of this" and consumers "buy less of this." When something is abundant, prices fall, signaling the opposite. No one needs to understand the whole system; prices coordinate everyone's actions automatically.

The law of demand says that when prices rise, people buy less. The law of supply says that when prices rise, producers make more. Where these two forces meet, we find equilibrium—the price that clears the market.

Shifts vs Movements

When the price of a good changes, we move along the existing curves. But other factors can shift the entire curve. A drought shifts the supply curve left (less available at every price). A viral trend shifts the demand curve right (more wanted at every price).

Demand shifts from changes in income, preferences, population, prices of substitutes/complements, or expectations. Supply shifts from changes in input costs, technology, number of sellers, or regulations.

Reading the Market

If price rises and quantity rises, demand increased. If price rises and quantity falls, supply decreased. The pattern tells you which curve shifted. Markets constantly adjust as millions of factors change, with prices doing the coordination.

Price Elasticity

Not all goods respond equally to price changes. Elastic demand means buyers are sensitive to price—a small price increase causes a large drop in quantity demanded. Inelastic demand means buyers keep buying despite price changes.

Necessities (insulin, gasoline) tend to be inelastic—you need them regardless of price. Luxuries with many substitutes (fancy coffee, brand-name goods) tend to be elastic—you'll switch if the price rises.

Who Pays the Tax?

When a tax is imposed, who bears the burden depends on elasticity. If demand is inelastic, consumers pay most of the tax through higher prices. If supply is inelastic, producers bear it through lower profits. The more elastic side can "escape" the tax by changing their behavior.

Markets in Action

Surge Pricing: Uber's prices rise when demand exceeds supply. This isn't just profit-seeking—it signals to more drivers "come work now" and to some riders "maybe wait or use alternatives." The higher price helps clear the market.

Housing Markets: When cities restrict new construction (limiting supply) while jobs grow (increasing demand), prices rise sharply. The solution isn't price controls (which create shortages) but allowing more supply.

Minimum Wage: A minimum wage is a price floor. If set above equilibrium, it creates surplus labor (unemployment). The debate is about whether the benefits to employed workers outweigh the costs to those who lose or don't get jobs.

Market Failures

Markets aren't perfect. Externalities (pollution), public goods (national defense), information asymmetries (used cars), and monopolies can prevent markets from reaching efficient outcomes. Understanding supply and demand helps identify when and how markets fail, and what interventions might help.

Key Insights

Prices coordinate without a coordinator. No one needs to understand the whole economy. Prices signal scarcity and abundance, guiding individual decisions toward collective efficiency. This decentralized coordination is remarkably powerful.

Equilibrium is dynamic. Markets constantly adjust to new information. There's no "right" price carved in stone—equilibrium shifts as conditions change. Price controls that fight this adjustment create shortages or surpluses.

Elasticity determines impact. How markets respond to changes depends on how easily buyers and sellers can adjust. Understanding elasticity is key to predicting the effects of taxes, regulations, and other interventions.

Where This Applies

Business: Pricing strategies, market analysis, forecasting. Policy: Taxes, subsidies, price controls, regulations. Investing: Understanding market dynamics and valuations. Daily life: Why prices change, when to buy, how wages work.

Quantum Mechanics

The strange rules of the very small: where particles can be in two places at once.

What is Quantum Mechanics?

Quantum mechanics describes how the universe works at the smallest scales—atoms, electrons, and photons. At this level, the intuitive rules of everyday physics break down. Particles don't have definite positions until you measure them. They can tunnel through walls. And they seem to "know" when they're being watched.

Schrödinger's Cat

The famous thought experiment: a cat in a box with a quantum-triggered poison is, according to quantum mechanics, both alive AND dead until you open the box to look. This isn't just philosophy—it's how particles actually behave. The act of measurement forces the universe to "decide."

Quantum mechanics isn't just theoretical—it's the foundation of modern electronics, lasers, MRI machines, and soon quantum computers. Understanding it means understanding the operating system of reality itself.

Quantum Tech You Use Daily

Transistors: Every computer chip relies on quantum tunneling—electrons jumping through barriers they classically couldn't pass. Your phone has billions of transistors, each exploiting quantum effects.

LED Lights: LEDs emit light when electrons drop between quantum energy levels. The specific colors depend on the quantum structure of the materials.

MRI Machines: Magnetic resonance imaging works by flipping the quantum spin of hydrogen atoms in your body and detecting when they flip back. It's quantum mechanics creating detailed images of your organs.

The Measurement Problem

Before measurement, a quantum particle is described by a "wave function" spread across space. Measurement "collapses" this wave function to a single point. But what counts as a measurement? Does it require consciousness? A machine? This question remains one of physics' deepest mysteries.

Double-Slit Experiment

The most beautiful experiment in physics. Fire particles one at a time through two slits. If particles were just particles, you'd see two bands. Instead, you get an interference pattern—proving each particle goes through BOTH slits as a wave.

Heisenberg's Uncertainty

You can't know both a particle's position and momentum precisely. The more precisely you know one, the less precisely you can know the other. This isn't a measurement limitation—it's a fundamental property of nature.

Understanding Quantum Reality

Wave Functions: A particle's state is described by a wave function ψ (psi). The square of this wave gives the probability of finding the particle at each location. Before measurement, the particle is "smeared out" across all possible positions.

Quantum Tunneling: A particle can appear on the other side of a barrier it doesn't have energy to climb over. This happens because the wave function doesn't stop at barriers—it decays exponentially through them, giving a small but real probability of appearing beyond.

Entanglement: Two particles can become "entangled" so that measuring one instantly affects the other, regardless of distance. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance" but it's been proven real. It doesn't allow faster-than-light communication, but it enables quantum cryptography and quantum computing.

Interpretations

Physicists agree on the math but disagree on what it means. Does the wave function "really" collapse? Are there parallel universes for each possibility? Is consciousness involved? These are the interpretations: Copenhagen, Many-Worlds, pilot wave, and more. The equations work regardless of which story you prefer.

Key Insights

Reality is probabilistic. At the quantum level, outcomes aren't determined until they happen. The universe genuinely doesn't "decide" until measurement occurs. This isn't ignorance—it's how reality works.

Observation affects reality. The act of measuring changes what you're measuring. In quantum mechanics, you can't be a passive observer—you're always a participant in the experiment.

Everything is waves. Not just light, but electrons, atoms, even you have wave properties. The wavelengths are just too small to notice at human scales. But at atomic scales, wave behavior dominates.

Where This Applies

Electronics: Semiconductors, transistors, lasers. Chemistry: How atoms bond and react. Future Tech: Quantum computers, quantum cryptography. Philosophy: The nature of reality, determinism, consciousness.

Cognitive Biases

The systematic errors in human thinking: why our brains deceive us.

Shortcuts That Mislead

Our brains evolved to make quick decisions with limited information. These mental shortcuts—heuristics—usually work well enough. But they also create predictable errors called cognitive biases. Understanding these biases helps us think more clearly and make better decisions.

Why Biases Exist

Evolution optimized our brains for survival, not truth. Fast, approximate thinking that's occasionally wrong beats slow, accurate thinking that gets you eaten. Our ancestors who jumped at shadows survived more often than those who carefully analyzed whether that rustle was a predator.

Cognitive biases aren't random mistakes—they're systematic patterns. The same bias affects most people in similar ways. This predictability means we can study them scientifically and, with effort, partially counteract them.

The Framing Effect

The same information can lead to different decisions depending on how it's presented. "90% survival rate" sounds better than "10% mortality rate"—even though they're identical. Our brains process gains and losses differently.

Framing affects everything from medical decisions to consumer choices. Marketers exploit this constantly: "95% fat-free" sells better than "5% fat." Being aware of framing helps you focus on the actual information rather than its packaging.

Loss Aversion

Losses loom larger than gains. Losing $100 feels worse than gaining $100 feels good—by about 2x according to research. This asymmetry means negative frames are often more powerful than positive ones. Fear of loss motivates more than hope of gain.

Framing Effect Demo

See how the same situation leads to different choices depending on how it's framed. This is based on the famous "Asian Disease Problem" by Tversky and Kahneman.

A disease outbreak will affect 600 people. Two programs are proposed:

Program A

200 people will be saved.

400 people will die.

Program B

1/3 chance all 600 saved, 2/3 chance none saved.

1/3 chance nobody dies, 2/3 chance all 600 die.

What research shows:

With positive framing, ~72% choose A (certainty). With negative framing, ~78% choose B (the gamble). Same outcomes, different choices. We're risk-averse for gains but risk-seeking to avoid losses.

Confirmation Bias

We seek out information that confirms what we already believe and ignore or dismiss evidence that contradicts it. This isn't conscious dishonesty—it's automatic. We genuinely find confirming evidence more compelling and memorable.

Confirmation bias explains why debates rarely change minds. Each side sees the same evidence as supporting their position. It's why first impressions are so sticky, why stereotypes persist, and why smart people can believe false things.

The Backfire Effect

Sometimes correcting misinformation makes people believe it more strongly. Challenging core beliefs can feel like a personal attack, triggering defensive reasoning. The most effective way to change minds is usually indirect— asking questions rather than presenting contradicting evidence.

Confirmation Bias Test

Can you figure out the rule? This is Wason's 2-4-6 task. Most people get it wrong because they only test confirming examples.

These numbers follow a rule:

2, 4, 6

Test if your sequence follows the rule:

What's the rule?

The actual rule:

Any three increasing numbers

Most people guess "even numbers" or "add 2" because they only test sequences like 8, 10, 12 or 20, 22, 24—which all follow the real rule too. To find the rule, you need to test sequences that would disprove your hypothesis, like 1, 2, 3 or 5, 10, 15.

More Common Biases

Availability Heuristic: We judge probability by how easily examples come to mind. Plane crashes feel more likely than car crashes because they're more memorable, even though cars are far more dangerous.

Hindsight Bias: After something happens, we feel we "knew it all along." This makes us overconfident in our predictive abilities and prevents us from learning from surprises.

Dunning-Kruger Effect: The less you know, the more you overestimate your knowledge. Experts doubt themselves; novices are confident. True competence brings awareness of how much you don't know.

Bias Blind Spot

The most meta bias: we see biases in others but not in ourselves. Even knowing about biases doesn't make us immune. The best defense is building systems and habits that counteract bias, rather than trusting our ability to think clearly in the moment.

Key Insights

Biases are universal. They're not signs of stupidity—they're features of how human cognition works. Smart people are just as susceptible, sometimes more so because they're better at rationalizing.

Awareness isn't enough. Knowing about biases doesn't prevent them. You need explicit debiasing techniques: consider alternatives, seek disconfirming evidence, use checklists, get outside perspectives.

Systems beat willpower. Instead of trying to think better in the moment, design environments and processes that reduce bias. Blind review processes, pre-commitment strategies, and diverse teams all help.

Where This Applies

Decisions: Major life choices, investments, hiring. Communication: Persuasion, negotiation, marketing. Science: Research design, peer review, replication. Technology: AI fairness, recommendation systems, UI design.

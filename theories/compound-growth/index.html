Compound Growth

The eighth wonder of the world: how small gains become enormous over time.

The Magic of Compounding

Einstein allegedly called compound interest the "eighth wonder of the world." Whether or not he said it, the principle is profound: when growth builds on itself, small rates create massive outcomes given enough time. A penny that doubles every day for a month becomes over $10 million.

Linear vs Exponential

Linear growth adds a fixed amount each period. Exponential growth multiplies by a fixed rate. The difference is dramatic: add $100/year for 30 years and you have $3,000. Grow $100 at 10%/year for 30 years and you have $1,745. The percentage wins despite looking smaller at first.

Compounding applies everywhere: investment returns, skill development, relationship building, knowledge acquisition. Understanding exponential growth—and its counterpart, exponential decay—is essential for long-term thinking.

The Rule of 72

Want to know how long until your money doubles? Divide 72 by the interest rate. At 7% growth, your money doubles in about 10 years (72 ÷ 7 ≈ 10). At 12%, it doubles in 6 years. This simple rule gives surprisingly accurate estimates.

The rule works because ln(2) ≈ 0.693, and 72 is close to 69.3 while being conveniently divisible by many numbers. It's a mental math shortcut that investors and financial planners use constantly.

The Flip Side

The Rule of 72 also works for decay. If inflation is 3%, prices double every 24 years. If a currency depreciates at 6%, it loses half its value in 12 years. Understanding exponential decay is just as important as understanding growth.

Small Differences, Big Outcomes

A 1% difference in returns might not seem like much, but over decades it's enormous. $10,000 growing at 7% for 30 years becomes $76,000. At 8%, it becomes $100,000. That single percentage point difference is $24,000—more than twice the original investment.

This is why fees matter so much in investing. A fund with 1% higher fees might seem similar to a cheaper alternative, but over a career of investing, that 1% could cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars.

The Patience Tax

Compounding rewards patience and punishes impatience. If you withdraw 10% of your gains each year instead of reinvesting them, your long-term wealth is dramatically reduced. The real power of compounding only emerges when you leave the money alone to grow.

Compounding Everywhere

Investing: Starting early matters more than starting big. Someone who invests $5,000/year from age 25-35 (10 years) will have more at 65 than someone who invests $5,000/year from 35-65 (30 years), at the same rate. Time beats money.

Skills: Getting 1% better at something each day means you're 37 times better after a year. Knowledge compounds too—what you learn today makes tomorrow's learning easier. This is why consistent practice beats occasional cramming.

Relationships: Small acts of kindness compound into deep relationships. Small acts of neglect compound into distance. The daily choices matter more than the grand gestures.

Negative Compounding

Debt compounds against you. Credit card debt at 20% interest doubles in under 4 years. This is why paying off high-interest debt is often the best "investment" you can make—it's a guaranteed return equal to the interest rate.

Key Insights

Time is the biggest factor. Starting early is more powerful than starting with more money. A 20-year-old investing $100/month will likely beat a 40-year-old investing $500/month by retirement.

Small rates matter. A 1% difference seems trivial but compounds into enormous differences. Minimize fees, maximize returns, and be patient.

Compounding works both ways. It builds wealth when you save and destroys it when you borrow. Understanding this asymmetry is financially crucial.

Where This Applies

Finance: Savings, investments, loans, inflation. Career: Skills, knowledge, reputation building. Health: Exercise benefits, habit formation. Business: Customer acquisition, brand building, market share.

Sound & Waves

How vibrations become music, speech, and everything you hear.

What Are Sound Waves?

Sound is vibration traveling through matter—air, water, or solids. When something vibrates (a guitar string, your vocal cords, a speaker cone), it pushes and pulls the surrounding air molecules, creating waves of compression and rarefaction that your ears interpret as sound.

In Space, No One Can Hear You

Sound needs a medium to travel through. In the vacuum of space, there's nothing to vibrate—so there's no sound. The iconic explosions in sci-fi movies? They'd be completely silent in reality.

Every sound has three key properties: frequency (pitch—how fast the wave oscillates), amplitude (volume—how much energy the wave carries), and timbre (tone quality—what makes a violin sound different from a flute playing the same note).

Frequency

Cycles per second (Hz). Higher frequency = higher pitch

Sound in the Real World

Human Hearing: We can hear frequencies from about 20 Hz (a deep rumble) to 20,000 Hz (a piercing whistle). As we age, we lose high-frequency sensitivity. Dogs can hear up to 65,000 Hz; bats use frequencies up to 200,000 Hz for echolocation.

Musical Notes: Middle C on a piano vibrates at 261.6 Hz. The A above middle C (the note orchestras tune to) is 440 Hz. Each octave doubles the frequency—A5 is 880 Hz, A6 is 1760 Hz.

Doppler Effect: When an ambulance passes you, the siren seems to drop in pitch. As it approaches, the sound waves are compressed (higher frequency); as it recedes, they're stretched (lower frequency). This same effect lets astronomers measure how fast stars are moving.

Breaking the Sound Barrier

Sound travels at about 343 m/s (767 mph) in air at sea level. When a jet exceeds this speed, it creates a sonic boom—a shock wave of compressed air. The "crack" of a whip is a tiny sonic boom from the tip moving faster than sound.

Wave Interference

When two waves meet, they combine. If their peaks align, they reinforce each other (constructive interference). If a peak meets a trough, they cancel out (destructive interference). This creates "beats"—a pulsing effect when two similar frequencies interact.

Fourier Analysis: Decoding Sound

One of mathematics' most beautiful discoveries: any periodic wave can be built by adding sine waves together. A square wave? It's an infinite sum of odd harmonics. Joseph Fourier proved this in 1822, and it revolutionized physics and engineering.

Your phone's voice recognition, MP3 compression, earthquake analysis, MRI imaging—they all use Fourier transforms to break complex signals into simple frequency components. It's the mathematical Swiss Army knife of signal processing.

Your Ear Does This Too

Your cochlea (inner ear) is essentially a biological Fourier analyzer. Different positions along its spiral respond to different frequencies, separating the complex sound wave into its component frequencies before sending that information to your brain.

Key Insights

Everything is vibration. Sound, heat, light, even matter at its deepest level—all involve waves and oscillations. Understanding waves gives you insight into countless phenomena.

Simple rules, complex results. Just by adding sine waves, you can build any sound imaginable. The rich timbre of a symphony orchestra? It's all sine waves, combined in infinitely varied ways.

Interference is everywhere. Noise-canceling headphones, radio dead spots, iridescent soap bubbles—all examples of wave interference. Once you understand it, you see it everywhere.

Where This Applies

Music: Instruments, synthesizers, audio engineering. Medicine: Ultrasound imaging, hearing aids. Engineering: Sonar, radar, vibration analysis. Telecommunications: Radio, Wi-Fi, fiber optics.

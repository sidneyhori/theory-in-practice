Quantum Computing

Computing with the strange rules of quantum mechanics.

What Is Quantum Computing?

Classical computers use bits—zeros and ones. A bit is either 0 or 1, never both. Quantum computers use qubits, which can be 0, 1, or a superposition of both simultaneously. This isn't just "0.5"—it's genuinely both states at once until measured.

Exponential Power

One qubit holds two states. Two qubits hold four. Three qubits hold eight. With n qubits, you can represent 2^n states simultaneously. Just 300 qubits could hold more states than there are atoms in the visible universe—that's the power of quantum parallelism.

Quantum computers aren't just faster classical computers. They solve certain problems in fundamentally different ways, using quantum effects like superposition, entanglement, and interference to explore many possibilities simultaneously.

Quantum Gates

Just as classical computers use logic gates (AND, OR, NOT), quantum computers use quantum gates. But quantum gates are reversible and work on amplitudes, not just bits. The Hadamard gate (H) is fundamental—it creates superposition from a definite state.

Hadamard (H): Transforms |0⟩ into (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2 (equal superposition) and |1⟩ into (|0⟩ - |1⟩)/√2. Apply H twice and you're back where you started.

Pauli-X: The quantum NOT gate. Flips |0⟩ to |1⟩ and vice versa. It's a 180° rotation around the X-axis of the Bloch sphere.

Reversibility

Unlike classical gates (AND loses information), quantum gates are always reversible. Every operation can be undone. This is required by quantum mechanics and enables quantum algorithms to "uncompute" intermediate steps.

What Quantum Computers Can Do

Cryptography: Shor's algorithm can factor large numbers exponentially faster than classical computers, threatening RSA encryption. But quantum key distribution offers theoretically unbreakable security.

Simulation: Richard Feynman originally proposed quantum computers to simulate quantum systems—molecules, materials, chemical reactions. Classical computers struggle because the complexity grows exponentially; quantum computers handle it naturally.

Optimization: Many important problems (logistics, finance, machine learning) involve finding the best solution among huge numbers of possibilities. Quantum algorithms like Grover's search offer quadratic speedups.

Quantum Advantage

Quantum computers aren't faster at everything—they're fundamentally different. For many tasks (browsing the web, word processing), classical computers will always be better. Quantum computers shine at specific problems where quantum parallelism and interference provide an advantage.

The Challenges

Decoherence: Qubits are incredibly fragile. Any interaction with the environment causes "decoherence"—the quantum states collapse into classical ones. This is why quantum computers need extreme isolation, often near absolute zero temperature.

Error Correction: Because qubits are noisy, quantum computers need error correction. But measuring a qubit destroys its state, so quantum error correction is complex—requiring many physical qubits per "logical" qubit.

Key Insights

Different, not just faster. Quantum computers process information in a fundamentally different way. They're not "super-fast classical computers"—they're a new paradigm that excels at specific problem types.

Superposition enables parallelism. A quantum computer can explore many solutions simultaneously, then use interference to amplify correct answers and cancel wrong ones. This is the heart of quantum algorithms.

We're still early. Today's quantum computers have dozens to a few thousand qubits, are noisy, and can only run short computations. Fault-tolerant quantum computers that outperform classical ones at useful tasks are still years away.

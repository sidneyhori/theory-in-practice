Game Theory

The mathematics of strategic decision-making: from pricing wars to social dilemmas.

What is Game Theory?

Game theory is the study of strategic decision-making—situations where your best choice depends on what others do. It's not about board games; it's about any situation where multiple people (or companies, or countries) make choices that affect each other's outcomes.

The Coffee Shop War

Two coffee shops open across the street from each other. If one lowers prices, customers switch to them. The other shop responds by lowering prices too. Soon both have low prices and low profits—even though both would prefer to keep prices high! This "race to the bottom" happens in gas stations, airlines, and streaming services.

You see game theory everywhere once you know what to look for. Social media algorithms, salary negotiations, international trade deals—all strategic games where success depends on anticipating others' moves.

Game Theory in Business

Pricing strategy: Airlines don't set prices in a vacuum. When Delta drops fares to Miami, United has to decide: match the price (keep customers but lower margins) or stay high (lose some customers but profit more on each ticket). The result? Airlines increasingly use algorithms to adjust prices in real-time, creating a continuous pricing game.

Advertising wars: Coke and Pepsi both spend billions on advertising. If both stopped, they'd each save money and probably keep similar market share. But neither can stop unilaterally—the other would grab customers. So both keep spending. This is why mature industries often have massive marketing budgets that might seem "wasteful."

Streaming exclusives: Netflix, Disney+, and HBO Max each sign exclusive content deals. From a viewer's perspective, having everything on one platform would be better. But from each company's view, exclusives are how you differentiate. The result: fragmented content and subscribers juggling multiple services.

Nash Equilibrium

A situation where no player can improve their outcome by changing strategy alone. Named after mathematician John Nash (portrayed in "A Beautiful Mind"), this concept explains why certain competitive patterns persist—even when everyone would be better off cooperating. The equilibrium is stable because any unilateral change makes you worse off.

Key Insights

The "rational" choice can be the wrong choice. In the Prisoner's Dilemma, defecting seems smart—it's better for you no matter what the other person does. But when both players think this way, both lose. This explains arms races, advertising wars, and why group projects sometimes fall apart.

Reputation is currency. In one-shot games, betrayal might pay off. But when you'll see someone again? Your past actions follow you. This is why reviews matter on Amazon and Yelp, why suppliers maintain quality with repeat customers, and why businesses protect their reputation even when cutting corners would be profitable short-term.

Simple rules can beat complex strategies. In computer tournaments run by political scientist Robert Axelrod, the simple "tit-for-tat" strategy (cooperate first, then copy your opponent) consistently beat sophisticated strategies. The lesson: be nice, be forgiving, be retaliatory when needed, and be predictable.

Where This Applies

Negotiations: Know when you're in a one-shot vs. repeated game. Pricing: Understand why price wars start and how to avoid them. Partnerships: Build trust through consistent cooperation over time. Competition: Anticipate competitor reactions before making moves.
